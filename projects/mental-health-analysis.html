Global Suicide Rate Analysis

Examining Trends, Gender Disparities, and Economic Correlations (1985-2016)

Mental Health Data Analysis Statistical Modeling Global Health Trends

Executive Summary

This project analyzes global suicide rates from 1985 to 2016, examining trends over time, gender differences, and potential correlations with economic factors such as GDP. The analysis provides insights into the complex patterns of suicide rates across different regions and demographic groups.

Key Achievement

Conducted comprehensive analysis of 31 years of global suicide data, identifying significant gender disparities and regional variations while determining that economic prosperity (measured by GDP) shows no significant correlation with suicide rates.

Key Findings

Global Trends (1985-2016)

The analysis revealed significant patterns in global suicide rates over the 31-year period:

  • Suicide rates worldwide increased until 2002, then began decreasing despite growing global population
  • The highest number of suicides (256,095) occurred in 2002
  • The highest rate per 100k population was recorded in 1995 at approximately 15.7
  • By 2015, the global rate had decreased to about 11.1 per 100k population

Global suicide rates per 100k population (1985-2016)

Gender Disparities

The analysis found striking differences between male and female suicide rates globally. Males have a suicide rate approximately 4 times higher than females (20 per 100k for males versus 5.4 per 100k for females). Male suicide rates peaked in 1995 and then steadily decreased, while female suicide rates have been consistently decreasing since 1985.

Comparison of male and female suicide rates (1985-2016)

Regional Variations

European and Asian regions show approximately double the suicide rates compared to North America and Australia. Lithuania had the highest recorded suicide rate, with 68.14 per 100k for males (roughly 5 times higher than the female rate of 13.03). Data for Africa and parts of South America is limited, highlighting gaps in global mental health monitoring.

Economic Correlation Analysis

A key focus of this research was examining the potential relationship between economic prosperity (measured by GDP) and suicide rates:

Scatter plot showing relationship between GDP and suicide rates

No Significant GDP Correlation

Statistical analysis found no significant correlation between a country's GDP (both annual and per capita) and its suicide rates. The regression model showed very low explanatory power (R-squared of 0.007374), indicating GDP explains very little of the variance in suicide rates. Visual representations confirmed the lack of pattern or correlation between GDP metrics and suicide rates across countries.

Statistical Methodology

The analysis employed several statistical approaches:

  • Regression Analysis - Linear regression models were used to test the relationship between GDP metrics and suicide rates
  • Correlation Coefficients - Pearson's r was calculated to measure the strength of association between economic indicators and suicide rates
  • R-squared Analysis - The coefficient of determination (0.007374) indicated that GDP explains less than 1% of the variance in suicide rates

Data Sources

The analysis utilized comprehensive datasets from:

  • World Health Organization - Global suicide statistics from 1985-2016
  • World Bank - GDP data (annual and per capita) for corresponding countries and years
  • United Nations - Population data for calculating per capita rates

Applications & Implications

This analysis offers valuable insights for mental health policy and intervention strategies:

Gender-Specific Interventions

The significant gender disparity suggests the need for targeted mental health approaches that address the unique factors affecting males and females.

Regional Focus

The higher rates in European and Asian regions indicate where mental health resources might be most urgently needed.

Beyond Economic Factors

The lack of GDP correlation suggests that mental health interventions should focus on social, cultural, and healthcare factors rather than assuming economic growth alone will improve outcomes.

Strategic Insight

The findings challenge the assumption that economic development automatically leads to better mental health outcomes. Countries at all economic levels need comprehensive mental health strategies that address cultural factors, healthcare access, and gender-specific approaches.

Conclusion & Future Work

This analysis provides valuable insights into global suicide trends over a 31-year period, highlighting significant gender disparities and regional variations. The lack of correlation with GDP underscores the complex, multifaceted nature of suicide as a public health issue that extends beyond economic factors.

Future Directions

This research could be extended in several promising directions:

  • Incorporate additional socioeconomic variables beyond GDP (e.g., income inequality, unemployment rates)
  • Analyze the impact of mental health services availability and accessibility across different regions
  • Examine cultural and social factors that might explain the significant gender disparities
  • Develop predictive models that incorporate multiple variables to better understand suicide risk factors
  • Address data gaps in regions with limited reporting, particularly in Africa and parts of South America
